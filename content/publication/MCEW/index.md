abstract: Increased wildfire, declining snowpack, and forest beetle outbreaks are raising the awareness and need for comprehensive forest management strategies that satisfy long term water quantity and water quality needs . Forest management activities can alter the soil nutrient pools and affect the timing and magnitude of stream water quantity and quality. We investigated the impact of contemporary forest management activities, including clear-cutting and thinning, on water yield and stream nitrogen and phosphorus dynamics in a quarter-century-long (1992-2016) paired and nested watershed study in the interior Pacific Northwest, US. Monthly water samples were collected and analyzed for total Kjeldhal nitrogen (TKN), total available nitrogen (TAN), nitrate + nitrite (NO3+NO2), total phosphorus (TP) and orthophosphate (OP) concentrations throughout the study period. Five years of calibration data, 4 years of post-road construction, 6 years of PH-I , and 9 years of PH-II data were analyzed using a before-after, control-impact paired series (BACIPS) study design. We found statistically significant increases in stream NO3+NO2 and OP loading from the paired and nested watersheds following timber harvest treatments. This increase in nutrient export was attributed to increases in both streamflow and concentrations following harvest. Streamflow increased the greatest following clear-cut practices (33.4% at W1 during PH-I) with strong responses in stream NO3+NO2 concentration (0.28 mg-N.L-1 and 0.33 mg-N.L-1 during PH-I and PH-II respectively at W1). Downstream cumulative watersheds displayed smaller relative increases in the stream NO3+NO2 and OP likely because of the instream dilution and nutrient assimilation effects. Interestingly, the undisturbed control watershed displayed similar increasing trends in concentration, streamflow, and export of NO3+NO2 and OP. This increase was, however, smaller in magnitude compared to the disturbed watersheds and is potentially driven by changing hydrologic responses from forests in rain-on-snow elevation bands. In summary, we found that the contemporary forest management activities altered the stream water quality by significantly increasing the stream NO3+NO2 and OP concentrations as well as loads from the paired and nested watershed.
