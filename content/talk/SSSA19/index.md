abstract: Forests are important non-point sources for phosphorus (P) release to surface waters. Fire suppression practices in lake Tahoe basin has resulted in excessive accumulation of forest litter layer containing large pool of phosphorus. Such forest floor can potentially act as a long-term source of biologically available phosphorus that can be transported via either leaching, litter flow or by surface runoff to the nearby surface waters. This premise is supported by recent studies that have reported high inorganic phosphorus concentration (25 mg/L P) in overland/litter interflow (Miller et al., 2005) derived from the forest floor O horizons (Loupe et al., 2007). Due to the downstream risk of excessive nutrient load on water clarity in Lake Tahoe the objective of this study was to characterize the fate and transport of P in the upland volcanic and granitic forest-meadow systems. We used soil samples from both a volcanic and a granitic forest-meadow system to understand the P adsorption potential using the batch isotherm method. From these same locations, six intact soil cores (7.6 cm diameter) representing the top 15 cm of mineral soil were used to characterize the effect of P type (organic vs inorganic) and direction of flow (infiltration and exfiltration) on transport of P. In the experiment 6.6 cm of DI water/spiking solutions (at pH ~ 6.5) were applied to the cores mimicking typical spring precipitation totals. Overall, P losses was greatly influenced by the concentration of Fe and Al hydroxides in the soil. Soil adsorption capacity was well correlated with the leachate P concentration and P loss via exfiltration. The P concentration with DI water addition was best explained by water-extractable soil P however soil Bray P best described P leachate response with spiked P experiments. The study provides important insights on P retention dynamics on the fate and transport from forest-meadow systems and has important implications of management on downstream water quality.
